Content Management Systems (CMS)

This page collects link to articles and other resources about accessibility in Content Management Systems (CMS). There are two sides to the accessibility of a CMS: firstly, the content presented to the website user, and secondly, the tools available to content authors. This means that the accessibility of a CMS should take two sets of guidelines into account:

  • First, the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0,
  • second, the Authoring Tool Accessibility Guidelines (ATAG) 2.0.

Consequently, accessibility statements that only reference WCAG are insufficient.

Comparisons and Other Articles

There seem to be no recent comparisons of the accessibility features in content management systems. The comparisons below are outdated; they only show which systems took accessibility into account in the previous decade. (The articles below are listed in reverse chronological order.)

  • John Siebert: About CMS Accessibility, Web Axe, 2 September 2011.
    This articles briefly discusses WordPress, Drupal, Posterous and Joomla, and concludes that WordPress and Drupal would be the best out of these four systems.
  • Élie Sloïm: “Comparatif accessibilité des CMS : un point avant les RMLL, 1 July 2010.

Individual CMSs

WordPress, Drupal and Joomla are probably the three systems for which the greatest number of accessibility resources is available. However, discussions about CMS accessibility also mention many other systems. Below is a list of such systems, and of systems with an accesssibility statement. They are listed in alphabetical order; inclusion in this list does not imply endorsement of any kind.

CMS Made Simple

(Homepage: www.cmsmadesimple.org.) The Features page mentions "Accessibility WAI, WCGA, Section 508".

Ametys

(Homepage: www.ametys.org.) See the French blog post “Accessibilité, enjeux à court terme pour les collectivités locales et les sites publics” and the whitepaper Livre blanc : Guide pour la conception de sites web accessibles

Contao

(Homepage: contao.org.) Contao defines itself as an “Accessible Open Source Content Management System”. The project has a German accessibility and usability group.

DotClear

(Homepage: dotclear.org.) See “accessibility” in the Guided Tour and Dotclear News: Dotclear 2.7.

Drupal

(Homepage: www.drupal.org.) See: Accessibility Statement, Contribute to accessibility, Accessibility Group, Nomensa Accessible Media Player.

Accessibility Best Practices in Drupal Theming, DrupalCon 2008.

EzPublish

(Homepage: ez.no.) No accessibility statement or other accessibility-related information.

Joomla

(Homepage: www.joomla.org.) See Accessibility Statement.

See also Joshue O'Connor's book Joomla! Accessibility (2007).

Mezzanine

(Homepage: mezzanine.jupo.org.) The website has accessibility statement or other accessibility-related information.

Christophe Buffenoir : Créer un blog accessible avec Mezzanine, Capitole du Libre, November 2014.

Redaxo

(Homepage: www.redaxo.org.) Redaxo is a German CMS. Its website has no accessibility statement, but the (German) documentation contains an article about Accesskeys.
According to Stephan Heller, Redaxo gives developers full control over its templates, which is helpful when you want to make them accessible. See the discussion about content management systems on the website of the German organisation Webkrauts.

SPIP

(Homepage: www.spip.net.) This CMS is “incontournable” in discussions about content management in France.

Typo3

(Homepage: typo3.org.) This system is well-known in the German-speaking world. For information about its accessibility features, see for example the release notes of an older version.

Webdragon

The developers of this system claim(ed) that they had put a lot of effort in the accessibility of their CMS: see How accessible is your website's CMS? (15 November 2010).

WordPress

(Homepage: wordpress.org.) See WordPress Codex: Accessibility and Make WordPress Accessible (blog).
